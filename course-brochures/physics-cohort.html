About This Course
What is the Classical Mechanics Weekend?

Classical mechanics is the foundation of all physics. Before quantum mechanics, before relativity, before any of the exotic frontiers of modern physics — there are Newton's laws, kinematics, energy, and momentum. Mastering these isn't just useful for exams; it builds the physical intuition and problem-solving discipline that every serious physics student needs.


This weekend cohort covers the complete core of classical mechanics in two rigorous, accessible days. Day 1 is about motion and forces: kinematics (how things move), Newton's three laws (why they move), and the systematic problem-solving approach using free body diagrams. Day 2 adds energy and momentum: conservation laws that often give you elegant solutions to problems that would take pages with Newton's laws alone.


The teaching style is physical intuition first — we ask "what do you expect?" before we write an equation. This is the habit that separates students who truly understand mechanics from those who are just pattern-matching to formulae. Problem sets with full solutions are included for continued practice after the weekend.

Curriculum
Two Days, Two Themes
Day 1
Motion & Forces
  • Kinematics — displacement, velocity, acceleration — the language of motion; scalar vs. vector quantities; the four kinematic equations derived from first principles and their conditions of validity
  • Newton's three laws in depth — not just statements but deep physical understanding: what inertia means, what force really is, why the third law pairs are never on the same object, and the common misconceptions that kill problem-solving
  • Friction, normal force & tension — the contact forces that appear in almost every mechanics problem; static vs. kinetic friction, why the normal force is always perpendicular, tension in strings over pulleys
  • Problem-solving method: FBDs & systematic approach — Free Body Diagrams as the universal problem-solving tool; a rigorous, repeatable framework for attacking any mechanics problem from first principles
Day 2
Energy & Momentum
  • Work, power & kinetic energy — the work-energy theorem as an alternative to Newton's second law; when the energy approach gives you a simpler solution; power as rate of energy transfer
  • Conservation of mechanical energy — potential energy (gravitational and spring), total mechanical energy, and when it's conserved; solving problems by tracking energy rather than forces
  • Momentum & impulse — linear momentum as a conserved quantity; impulse-momentum theorem; the connection between force, time, and change in motion
  • Collisions — elastic & inelastic — applying conservation of momentum and energy to collisions; perfectly elastic, perfectly inelastic, and the spectrum between; real-world collision problems
  • Rotational motion basics — angular displacement, velocity, and acceleration; torque as the rotational analogue of force; moment of inertia and rotational kinetic energy
