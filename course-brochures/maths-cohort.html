About This Course
What is Maths for ML — Power Weekend?

Machine learning is built on three mathematical pillars: calculus (for optimisation and backpropagation), linear algebra (for data representation and transformation), and probability & statistics (for reasoning under uncertainty and model evaluation). Most practitioners lack one or more of these, and that gap slowly limits what they can do.


This weekend cohort covers all three — deeply and clearly — in two focused days. It's designed for people who are already working with ML or AI and want to go deeper, or who are preparing for the AI & ML Engineering 12-Week program and want a solid mathematical foundation before it begins.


The teaching is visual and intuition-first throughout. Proofs and abstract formalism are deferred in favour of geometric and probabilistic understanding. By Sunday afternoon you'll be able to read the maths in ML papers without fear, understand why gradient descent converges, explain what PCA is doing, and solve probability problems of the kind that appear in ML interviews. A problem-solving marathon and personal study plan close out Day 2.

Curriculum
Two Days, Four Blocks
Day 1 — Morning
Calculus for Optimisation
  • Derivatives & partial derivatives — single-variable derivatives reviewed, then extended to multivariable functions; the partial derivative as "rate of change while holding others constant"
  • Gradient descent from scratch — implementing gradient descent step by step; the gradient vector as the direction of steepest ascent and why we go in the opposite direction
  • Chain rule & backpropagation intuition — the chain rule as the mechanism behind backpropagation; how gradients flow backwards through a computational graph
  • Multivariable optimisation — finding minima of functions of multiple variables; saddle points, local vs. global minima, and what this means for training neural networks
Day 1 — Afternoon
Linear Algebra
  • Vectors, matrices & transformations — vectors as geometric objects, matrices as transformations; the visual intuition that makes ML notation readable
  • Matrix multiplication — as composition of transformations; dot products, inner products, and why matrix operations are the language of neural networks
  • Eigenvalues & PCA — eigendecomposition explained geometrically; how PCA uses eigenvectors to find the directions of maximum variance in data
  • NumPy hands-on — implementing matrix operations, eigendecomposition, and PCA in NumPy; tying the session's maths to real ML code
Day 2 — Morning
Probability & Statistics
  • Probability basics — sample spaces, events, conditional probability, independence; common distributions (Gaussian, Bernoulli, Multinomial) and where they appear in ML
  • Expected value & variance — the mathematical definition of "average" and "spread"; why variance matters for model generalisation and regularisation
  • MLE & MAP estimation — Maximum Likelihood Estimation as the foundation of most ML training; the Bayesian extension via Maximum A Posteriori; how this connects to loss functions
  • Information theory basics — entropy as "uncertainty", KL divergence as "difference between distributions"; why these appear in VAEs, GANs, and language model training
Day 2 — Afternoon
Tying It Together
  • How all three areas combine in a neural network — a complete walkthrough of the mathematical story of a training step: linear algebra (forward pass), calculus (gradient computation), probability (loss function)
  • Reading an ML paper's maths section — a live session working through a real paper excerpt; applying what you've learned to actual research notation
  • Problem-solving marathon — a structured set of problems spanning all three areas, worked collaboratively; exam-style and interview-style questions
  • Personal study plan — based on your goals and which areas need more depth, a concrete continuation plan for going further in each subject

How does this compare to taking the three individual masterclasses?
The Power Weekend is more cohesive — it explicitly connects all three areas and ends with a unifying session on how they combine in a neural network. The individual masterclasses give more time to each topic. Both paths are valid.
Is this a prerequisite for the 12-Week AI & ML Engineering program?
No — the 12-Week program introduces the relevant maths as needed. However, attending the Power Weekend first means you arrive with stronger foundations and can focus on the ML applications rather than catching up on maths.
Do I need a calculator or any specific tools?
A scientific calculator is helpful for the probability section. For the NumPy session, you'll need a laptop with Python and NumPy installed — we send setup instructions beforehand.
