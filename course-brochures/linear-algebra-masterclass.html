About This Course
What is Linear Algebra for ML in 3 Hours?

If you've ever looked at a machine learning paper and seen a wall of matrices, indices, and summation signs and felt your brain switch off — this session is the fix. Linear algebra is the language that ML is written in, and once you develop a geometric visual intuition for it, the notation becomes transparent rather than opaque.


The session is structured around visual understanding first. A vector isn't just a list of numbers — it's an arrow in space. A matrix isn't just a grid — it's a transformation that stretches, rotates, and reflects space. Matrix multiplication isn't just a formula — it's the composition of two transformations. Once you see this, everything else follows naturally.


The third hour applies this intuition directly to machine learning: how the weights in a neural network layer are a matrix transformation, how PCA reduces dimensions by finding the directions of maximum variance (eigenvalues and eigenvectors), and how embeddings represent meaning as vectors in high-dimensional space. There's also a practical NumPy session where you implement key operations in code.

Curriculum
What You'll Cover, Hour by Hour
Hour 1
Vectors & Spaces
  • What a vector really is — not a list of numbers but a directed quantity in space; the geometric interpretation that makes everything else clear
  • Vector operations — addition (parallelogram rule), scalar multiplication (stretching/shrinking), and the dot product (projections and similarity); each explained geometrically
  • Basis vectors and span — how any vector in a space can be expressed as a combination of basis vectors; the concept of a coordinate system as a choice of basis
  • Linear independence — when a set of vectors provides genuinely new directions vs. being redundant; why this matters for dimensionality and PCA
Hour 2
Matrices as Transformations
  • Matrix multiplication as composition of transformations — the key insight: matrix-vector multiplication transforms a vector; matrix-matrix multiplication composes two transformations; why order matters
  • Transpose and inverse — geometric interpretations of both; when an inverse exists, what it means when it doesn't, and why these operations appear everywhere in ML
  • Determinant — what it actually means — the determinant as a scale factor for area/volume under a transformation; zero determinant as "squashing" to a lower dimension
  • Systems of equations — solving Ax = b geometrically; why this is equivalent to finding the intersection of hyperplanes; the connection to neural network layers
Hour 3
Eigenvalues, PCA & Neural Nets
  • Eigenvalues & eigenvectors visually — vectors that don't change direction under a transformation; how eigendecomposition reveals the "natural axes" of a matrix; visual demonstration
  • PCA — how dimensionality reduction works — Principal Component Analysis explained from eigenvectors; how it finds the directions of maximum variance and uses them to reduce dimensions
  • How weights in neural networks are matrices — each layer is a matrix multiplication followed by a non-linearity; the geometric interpretation of what a deep network is doing to its input
  • Practical NumPy session — implementing dot products, matrix multiplication, eigendecomposition, and a simple PCA in NumPy; the code that ties the session's ideas to real ML practice
