I've taught physics for years, and the single most reliable predictor of whether a student will struggle is whether they try to memorise equations before building intuition.

It doesn't work. And it makes physics miserable.

Here's the approach that actually works — and why it's so different from what most textbooks do.

## The wrong way to learn physics

Open almost any physics textbook and you'll find the same pattern:

1. Definition
2. Formula
3. Worked example
4. Problem set

Students learn to manipulate symbols. They get good at identifying which formula to use and plugging in numbers. They can solve textbook problems and fail completely when a problem is phrased slightly differently.

Why? Because they never built a *model* of what's actually happening. They learned calculation, not physics.

## What physical intuition actually is

Physical intuition is the ability to look at a situation — before you've written a single equation — and have a rough sense of what will happen and why.

- If I push a heavy box and a light box with the same force, which accelerates more? (The light one — same force, less mass)
- If I drop a feather and a bowling ball in a vacuum, which hits the ground first? (Same time — gravity accelerates all masses equally)
- If I double the length of a pendulum, what happens to its period? (It gets longer — but by how much? That requires more thought)

You should be able to reason through the first two without any equations. If you can't, you're missing the foundation that makes equations meaningful.

## How to build intuition: the approach that works

### 1. Observe before you calculate

Before any new topic, spend time just *watching* phenomena. Modern physics education has a huge advantage here: YouTube. There are extraordinary demonstrations of virtually every concept — Veritasium, PBS Space Time, 3Blue1Brown's physics content.

Watch a gyroscope precess before you learn the equations for angular momentum. Watch slow-motion footage of collisions before you study momentum conservation. The equations will be much easier to grasp once your brain has a visual model.

### 2. Ask "what would happen if?" constantly

The best question in physics is: what would change if I changed this one variable?

What happens to the force of gravity if I double the distance? (It quarters — the inverse square law)  
What happens to the kinetic energy if I double the speed? (It quadruples — KE scales with v²)  
What happens if there's no friction? (Objects keep moving forever — Newton's first law)

This kind of thinking — varying one thing and reasoning about the consequence — is how physicists actually think. It's also how you catch errors in calculations. If your formula says doubling the speed *halves* the kinetic energy, you know immediately something's wrong.

### 3. Use dimensional analysis

Before solving any problem, check that your answer *could* be right by checking its units.

Force is measured in Newtons (kg·m/s²). If your calculation for force has units of kg·m/s, you've made an error somewhere — because the units don't match.

Dimensional analysis won't tell you the right answer, but it will tell you when you definitely have the wrong one. Professional physicists use it constantly.

### 4. Estimate before you calculate

Fermi estimation — making rough order-of-magnitude calculations — is one of the most powerful physics skills there is, and almost no course teaches it explicitly.

How many piano tuners are in Delhi? How much energy does a car use in a year? How fast would you need to run to feel air resistance?

These questions don't have exact answers, but you can reason about them to within a factor of ten — and the process of doing so forces you to understand the underlying physics deeply.

Enrico Fermi was famous for this. The "Fermi problems" physicists pose to each other are a kind of sport.

## A concrete example: Newton's second law

Most students learn F = ma as a formula to use. Here's how to build intuition for it instead.

**Start with the concept:** Force causes acceleration. More force → more acceleration. More mass → less acceleration for the same force. That's the core idea. Write it in words before you write the equation.

**Check extreme cases:** What if force is zero? Then acceleration is zero — the object doesn't change its motion. What if mass is enormous? Then even a large force produces tiny acceleration. Both make sense.

**Make it physical:** Hold a pen. Now hold a textbook. Push both with the same force — your fingertip. Which moves more readily? The pen. That's F = ma. You've felt it.

**Now use the equation:** Once you have the intuition, the equation F = ma is just a precise statement of what you already understand. Calculations with it are easy because you can always sanity-check against your intuition.

## The subjects where intuition is hardest

Some areas of physics genuinely resist intuition because they don't match everyday experience.

**Quantum mechanics** operates on scales where our evolved intuition simply doesn't apply. Particles in superposition, entanglement, the double-slit experiment — these are deeply counterintuitive because we've never experienced them directly.

**Special relativity** is similar. Time dilation and length contraction are real, verified experimentally — but they feel wrong because we never move fast enough to notice them.

In these areas, the right approach is to trust the mathematics over your gut. The equations have been verified to extraordinary precision. Your intuition, built for everyday scales and speeds, can be misleading.

For classical mechanics, thermodynamics, and waves — the foundation of a physics education — intuition is everything. Build it first. The equations will follow.

## The payoff

Students who build intuition before calculation develop something rare: they become *surprised* when calculations give wrong answers, because the answer doesn't match what they expected. That surprise is a signal to go find the error.

Students who only calculate have no such anchor. They write down whatever comes out of the algebra and hope it's right.

Physics isn't about formulas. It's about understanding how the world behaves. The formulas are just the way we write that understanding down precisely.

Start with the world. The formulas will make sense.
